Measuring \(D_{ap}\)

We set out to measure the \(D_{ap}\) for PYO in the IDA grown biofilm based on a sensitive generator/collector (gc) measurement.

V1: Soak titration \(I_{gc}\) vs. [PYO]

Originally, we were just soaking a biofilm in a concentration of PYO and looking at how current increased. Recall, the main goal was to see if the relationship was linear or nonlinear. However, we realized that most of the signal is coming from the solution phase PYO, not necessarily PYO associated with the biofilm.

V2: Transfer equilibrium titration \(I_{gc}\) vs. \(I_{swv}\)

We decided to transfer the biofilm to a fresh reactor after soaking, so that we would be measuring biofilm associated PYO. In the course of monitoring the PYO equilibrate out of the biofilm into the fresh media, we realized it might be possible to measure \(D_m\). We no longer knew the concentration of PYO at the electrode, so we then adopted the SWV vs.Â GC measurement.

Typically, we waited for the PYO to equilibrate between the biofilm and the fresh solution. However, at this point, the solution concentration of PYO was no longer negligible, presenting the same problem as in V1 above.

V3: Transfer non-equilibrium titration \(I_{gc}\) vs. \(I_{swv}\)

Currently, the approach is to measure while PYO is equilibrating with the solution. By measuring before equilibrium, we can be confident that we are measuring biofilm associated PYO, and not solution phase PYO.

Measuring \(D_m\)

From monitoring PYO equilibration for \(D_{ap}\) V2, it seemed possible to measure the physical diffusion coefficient of PYO \(D_m\) from the consecutive SWVs taken to monitor the equilibration.

V1: SWV decays - \(p_1\) = \(I_0\)

In order to estimate \(D_m\) we needed to have an estimate of the initial current, \(I_0\). I assumed that the first point, would be a reasonable estimate. It is now clear that \(p_1\) is not a good estimate of \(I_0\) and that dramatically influences the \(D_m\) estimate.
